    BASIC PLOT
     High above London is a ship the size and shape of St Pancras railway station. On board, the Doctor and Iris Wildthyme are bargaining for their lives with creatures determined to infiltrate the Earth in the guise of characters from nineteenth-century

     Third.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    • Pg 192 "'Yes, I remember you telling me how it fell off that cliff face on Peladon all the way to the bottom and... Oh.' 'That,' said the Doctor darkly, 'hasn't happened yet.'" That's despite this book being clearly set immediately prior to The Three Doctors.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    • Despite its celebrity status, this isn't really a cock-up, as the Doctor's clearly referring to him telling Iris about the adventure, not the adventure itself. The novel's setting is made extremely clear later in the book; it just confused people back in the day when we were used to the back cover telling us exactly when each book was set.

    IN SUMMARY - Robert Smith?
     Verdigris is nonstop fun from beginning to end. The jokes are hilarious, simultaneously poking fun at the show from the seventies, sending up postmodernism, exploring what makes the character of the third Doctor tick and being a loving tribute to its source material. It's one of the few out and out comedies in the Doctor Who books range and it hits its targets with sublime pitch. Sit back, relax and enjoy.
