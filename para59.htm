Parallel 59
by Natalie Dallaire and Stephen Cole

Publisher: BBC
ISBN: 0 563 55590 4

     

    BASIC PLOT
     The Doctor, Compassion and Fitz land on a space station above Skale, a planet divided into Parallels, antagonistic towards one another. By the simple act of arriving, they destabilize a massive planet-wide defence and control system, and everything starts to go wrong. With Fitz lost in a land called Mechta, working his way through the female occupants, Compassion teams up with some unlikely renegades and the Doctor tries to convince everyone that he's really on their side. So, the Doctor asks Parallel 59, what do you do with your political prisoners?

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 34 "I want to be sure they really are as human as you and me." Given that Skale is an out-of-the-way planet on the verge of breaking into spaceflight, and the fact that all its inhabitants have two hearts, the world 'human' is appropriate neither in terms of their physical make-up, or their species in general. So why does Dam use the word?
    2. Pg 76 "So I talked to his doctor." All very well, but just a few pages earlier, Nikol declares that no one on Mechta is qualified to reference the medical files (page 67). So what, exactly, do the doctors do?
    3. Pg 166 "Inside the lifts were scores of people in the dark business suits of all Central employees, crammed into the metal box in silence. Their eyes were open but unseeing, their bodies twisted unnaturally with the compression, like corpses in a mass grave. The dead staff of Central, waiting for home time to come." Yes, it's an arresting image, but it makes no logical sense. The inhabitants of Mechta are all dreaming a computer-generated reality. The inhabitants of Central, meanwhile, don't exist, so there's no sense in them all compressing themselves in a lift: they should simply walk into Central and vanish, since they don't exist anyway. The only reason that they walk into this lift than goes nowhere is so that they can be found doing so.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Weird book. Lots of ideas, many of which are clever, and lots of symbolism about communism, which is impressive. But I could not, no matter how hard I tried, bring myself to care, even slightly, about any of the characters described in the book. They all have relationships with each other, they all care, they all love, they all hate and I don't give the slightest damn. Yes, when we read a Doctor Who book, we're supposed to care about him and his companions, and they're not bad, but, as presented here, they're hardly gripping reading material either. And you can't help but have the feeling, around page 200, that an awful lot of characters just lost their usefulness to the plot as they are blown away for seemingly little apparent reason. Somehow, written by the editor of the line - yes, even this Eric Saward of the book editors - his novel should have been so much more than this, a mission statement, if you will, and a clear definition of the Doctor's character, neither of which we get. So, yes, I'm interested in the story, vaguely. But I don't - even a little - care a single jot about anyone who appears in it.
