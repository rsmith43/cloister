Unnatural History
by Jonathan Blum & Kate Orman

Publisher: BBC
ISBN: 0 563 55576 9

     

    BASIC PLOT
     The TARDIS is plugging a scar in space-time that the Doctor carelessly left behind when he regenerated in San Francisco. And Sam vanishes into it. When the Doctor tracks her down in London, she's not quite the girl that he remembers, but they bundle back to America to sort the scar. And the Unnaturalist. And a Professor Daniel Joyce. And the Kraken. And the unicorns. And Faction Paradox. Or do they? None of this may ever have happened.

    DOCTOR
     Eighth.

    COMPANIONS
     Fitz Kreiner and Sam Jones, although the latter spends the vast majority of the book in a variety of 'Dark Sam' personas.

    MATERIALISATION CIRCUIT
     We don't see it happen at the beginning of the book. By the time the story starts, it's already appeared in San Francisco, October 2002, and has been used to plug the scar in the universe.

    PREPARATORY READING
     Alien Bodies is helpful, as is The Infinity Doctors. The 'Leakey Bible' also informs some of the plot and characterization, so The Nth Doctor is useful.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     It's OrmanBlum with the volume turned up to 15. Everything of their style is here, only moreso, and it begins - after a time - to get rather tiresome. There's a 'shock' line roughly every five pages (read it - you'll see what I mean) and the whole thing's just a little bit too clever for its own good. The goalposts keep moving so that the Doctor can be expiated from the charge of having created Dark Sam, and there's just so much going on. That said, expectations are high for Kate and Jon, so it becomes easy to criticise what would have been great from the vast majority of other books from this period. Some of the writing, turns of phrase and ideas are magnificent, and Dark Sam's an absolute dream - why couldn't we have had her instead of Blonde Sam? But there's a little too much self-indulgence when the plot incorporates aspects of the production notes from the Telemovie that practically no one has read, but somehow we're all expected to understand. And why, in a book meant to answer one of the big questions of the range, do we end up coming out with even more questions than we had when we went in?
