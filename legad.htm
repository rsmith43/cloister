    BASIC PLOT
     The Doctor receives a telepathic message from his first travelling companion, Susan, and travels to an Earth slowly recovering from the Dalek invasion. There he gets embroiled in local politics, which are being exacerbated by an old enemy. But not an old enemy as bad as the one lurking in Artefact DA-17...

    DOCTOR
     Eighth.

    COMPANIONS
     Susan, at whom the Doctor briefly glances.

    The Doctor also adds another cat (after the departure of Wolsey with Benny) to his travelling companions, not named at this point.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 2 "I lost my best friends and my first girlfriend that way." Okay, yes, the Dalek invasion was pretty brutal. However, Becca's Dad goes on to say "I were five then, younger than you." So he had his first girlfriend when he was five?
    2. Pg 7 "Her body collapsed under her, and she rolled into the bracken and bushes. Twigs ripped at her exposed skin and hair, and she yelped. Winded by the fall, she was determined to move on." No animals were harmed in the making of this novel; indeed all seven kittens that Becca has tucked into her jumper appear to survive the rather brutal fall that she's just taken. Which seems unlikely in the extreme.
    3. Pg 16 "She looked eighteen - if that. Her elfin face stared back at her in disgust, the hair cropped close to her head. She was in truth so much older, but she wouldn't look that way for several more centuries." Which is all very well - we're told that Susan uses make-up and padding to make her appear in her 50s - but doesn't square with the fact that she's clearly a lot older-looking than 18 in The Five Doctors.
    4. Pg 17 "Their three children had all been Dalek war orphans, adopted and raised as their own. She had loved - and still did love - Ian, Barbara and David Junior." There's something strange going on here: normally war orphans - as opposed to children adopted soon after birth when the mother chooses not to keep them - already have names and are often somewhat older than newborn or a couple of months, as children that young wouldn't survive long enough to be taken in for adoption. Generally in that situation, it's actually quite cruel to change a name that they are used to, so how come the names Ian, Barbara and David fit Susan like a glove?
    5. Pg 22 "It was - no surprise! - an unevaluated site, just a few miles from the main Dalek mining camp in Surrey." Except the main Dalek mining camp was in Bedfordshire according to The Dalek Invasion of Earth.
    6. Pg 23 "The skin on his face still itched from where he'd restored it, and his memory still pained him from the causes of those scars." This is what's just happened on Hirath, but the Doctor, lying semi-comatose at the end, is described by Anstaar as having 'cuts and grazes' (Pg 272), nothing that would cause scarring so bad as to need restorative treatment, particularly on a man who, at the end of Genocide, managed to sleep off weeks of torture and beatings in 28 hours. It's particularly galling, given that the Doctor enters a healing trance on page 233 of this very book.
    7. Pg 49 "Both her hearts thudded." Susan has two hearts. How odd. The Doctor had only one when he was in his first incarnation.
    8. Pg 105 "It wasn't - couldn't be - her grandfather? Was it even a Time Lord?" Susan fails to recognize the Master, despite seemingly having been aware of him in Time and Relative, and, more tellingly, having met him in The Five Doctors.
    9. Pg 138 "'Doktoro,' said a fresh voice, one that Donna had never heard before. 'Mi gojas ke vi estas tiu kiun mi bezonis por kompletigi la ludon.' The Doctor spun around to stare at the newcomer. 'Tiam kiam mi audis la nomon "Estro", mi opiniis ke tiu devas esti vi. Via vanteco estos la fino de vi, estro de malbonestroj.'" There's no excuse for the Esperanto anyway, but particularly when you remember that the TARDIS translation circuits should be slipping it all into English for the Doctor anyway.
    10. Pg 141 "I seem to have done something naughty. My people usually have a law that we must meet each other in a linear progression along out relative time-streams. But I've slipped back in regards to the Master - I've met him in two and a half bodies since this one." It's not a continuity cock-up per se, but could I just say that no one, no one, wanted this sort of thing to be made into canon? While it's true that this has generally appeared to be the case in the history of the show, there's been absolutely nothing concrete to back it up at any point. By writing this, Peel stricturalises and formalises every single Time Lord meeting ever. And we desperately didn't want him to.
    11. Pg 181 "Donna could remember Haldoran being gentle with his children." How? When? The whole point is that she was barren - she couldn't have any children - and it's established that he didn't have an heir, so there were certainly no children beforehand. She's never returned to Castle Haldoran until now, so can't have seen him with the children he's had since unless it was on a news broadcast, which surely she would know would be somewhat staged? So, in essence, how?
    12. Pg 184 "Donna shuddered at the thought. It was too much like the tales of zombies she'd heard as a teenager, and the idea of being an animated corpse with no knowledge of self, or of needs, scared her." So Donna heard tales of zombies as a teenager, as well, of course, of loads of stories about Daleks. How come she didn't hear anything of Robomen, then, and has to extrapolate to zombies to come up with something synonymous?
    13. Pg 193 "That's the reason they came to Earth in the first place - they wanted the metal present at its molten core." No they didn't! - they wanted to remove it and replace it with a travelling device. Is this an attempted retcon?
    14. Pg 240 "There were days yet before the official resignation." The implication of The Deadly Assassin is that the Master has been there for weeks at the very least while Goth puts his plans into place, not days.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. Becca's dad had his eye on the prize from the very beginning.
    2. They're just damned lucky kittens.
    3. It's extremely convincing make-up. And the Doctors, who would surely know that she shouldn't look like this, cover her embarrassment by not mentioning it at all. To be fair, Susan doesn't mention the Rassilon affair at all during the novel - only the Doctor thinks about it - but the novelisation of The Five Doctors clearly states that she's kidnapped from New London, and everything else - the name 'New London' and Susan and David's three children - are designed to accommodate the continuity from said novelisation.
    4. It's a faintly ridiculous image, but I have this picture in my head of David and Susan standing in front of a whole bunch of orphans shouting out 'Anyone here called Ian? Anyone called Barbara?' and so on. Maybe they did; there are worse ways of choosing children, I suppose.
    5. Presumably there were a number of mines across the country and Susan means the main one in that county, rather than the actual main one which isn't in that county at all. I'm pretty sure that's not what the intention of the line was, though.
    6. Presumably, after we left the Doctor at the end of Longest Day, he tripped on his way back to the TARDIS, and really smashed his face up. His painful memory of the causing of the scars, then, is actually him smarting because he was stupid enough not to look where he was putting his feet.
    7. Susan, being womb-born, as we discover in Lungbarrow, begins her life with two hearts. Of course, this doesn't make sense of the fact that later she thinks she can regenerate, and the womb-born in Lungbarrow, including Rassilon and the Other themselves, admit that they can't. Patience can in Cold Fusion, though, so we'll let this one go.
    8. Time and Relative implies that her memories have been tampered with, so you can skate around it there. If you go for the idea that The Five Doctors happened later in Susan's time-line, then there's no more problem. However, if you believe it happened earlier than this, one can only assume that she thought that Ainley and Delgado looked nothing whatsoever like each other.
    9. Time Lords can telepathically shut down each other's access to translation circuits in order to be pretentious. But if this is the case, then why in the blue blazes do they not speak in Old High Gallifreyan or something?
    10. If anyone ever needs to justify this, let's just say that the Doctor's wrong.
    11. Like Henry VIII, Haldoran had any number of bastard children before he married Donna, and she saw him with them. Would've been nice to have this information, though.
    12. Too many people had been related to people who were Robotised, and it became considered bad luck, or in poor taste, to discuss them.
    13. Susan's trying to confuse the Master, so she lies.
    14. Given the speed that things tend to run on Gallifrey, the gap between the official resignation and the actual standing down could be months, even years.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Well, to damn it with faint praise, it's better than War of the Daleks. Marginally. That Estro is the Master is so stunningly obvious it's a wonder we weren't told this from the first page. The language used is often quite awful ('freeing his mind again from its dreadful grip' (page 27) being one of the worst offenders). The villains are all, seemingly by definition, sadists, which has the unfortunate effect of making them also all two-dimensional cliches, while the politics on display is a bizarre mix of Kings and customer loyalty to nuclear power providers. And the Daleks are so useless, it's absolutely hysterical. On the bright side, Sam's not in it. And there's a good conversation on Pg 138.
