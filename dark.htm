    OLD FRIENDS AND OLD ENEMIES
     The Master, although chronologically this is his first appearance. He's named Koschei here, although he drops that name during the course of the novel.

    We also see Terileptils, Draconians and Alpha Centaurans.

    The setting features Adjudicators (first seen in Colony in Space), the remnants of the Earth Empire (seen throughout the NAs, culminating in So Vile A Sin) and the Federation which supplanted it (Curse of Peladon).

    NEW FRIENDS AND NEW ENEMIES
     Captain Gillian Sherwin, Ailla, Brokhyth, Koskhoth, Halekouth.

    The Veltrochni turn up again, most notably in Mission: Impractical and a parallel Koschei is later seen in Face of the Enemy.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS
     This one's a doozy:

    • Pg 17 "Somewhat surreally, the Terrileptil was standing between the pincers of a bulbous, midnight-blue winged scorpion roughly the size of a groundcar. In fact he was gently stroking the shutters over its eyes. [...] 'A broken leg would be simple enough in a human, but of course with a Xarax exoskeleton, the break is on the outside of the body.'" This is a pretty fair description of the Xarax seen on the cover of Dancing the Code... except that the natural form of the Xarax is nothing like this and in the scene on the cover they were impersonating human helicopters. As all those Xarax were destroyed, it's hard to imagine why this one would have taken to impersonating a human helicopter, circa 1973 (and the Darkheart hasn't acted on the Xarax yet and only turns species into humans or erases them altogether, so that can't be the reason).

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    • The Xarax's duties aboard the ship involved ariel movements and the helicopter design was generic enough for it to copy.

    IN SUMMARY - Robert Smith?
     The Dark Path is, with one exception, unrelentingly dull. It features sub-Star Trek settings and characters, poorly drawn regulars, masses of pointless continuity and is exceedingly tedious to read. Its single redeeming feature is Koschei, who lights up every scene he's in, making this book far more memorable than it deserves to be. Unfortunately, he's underused and there's little of interest to sustain the rest of the novel.
