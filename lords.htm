Lords of the Storm
by David A. McIntee

Publisher: Virgin
ISBN: 0 426 20460 3

     

    BASIC PLOT
     The Sontarans are luring the Rutans into a trap as part of their ever-ongoing war. They think they will win, but doing so will destroy a human colony utterly. The Doctor cannot fail to get involved.

    DOCTOR
     Fifth.

    COMPANIONS
     Turlough. Kamelion is mentioned in passing.

     Pg 7 The third moon of Indra is called Agni, named after the Indian God of Fire. It's not a connection, but a statuette of Agni is important to the plot of The Burning.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 157 "The Doctor pointed his own torch at them, flashing it on and off in a rapid but steady sequence of four long and three short, over and over. 'Just shine your torch into the eyes of anyone under control, and flash your torch in this polka beat until they come round.'" Sorry: it's not a Polka. It's closer to a Bolero, but a very unusual one, since it's in five beats in a bar.
    2. On Pg 175, the Sontarans are trying to get a positive ID on the Doctor. They show Jahangir the Fourth, Third and Second Doctors, but completely ignore the Sixth, despite the fact that their footage of Doc 2 is from The Two Doctors. Presumably this is because this is a Fifth Doctor book, but it makes no sense from their point of view.
    3. Pg 204 Lo's serial number is 44327 squared. Or 1964882929 to normal beings. Surely Sontaran serial numbers are not all square numbers. This is just bizarre.
    4. Carelessness sets in; the proof-reading has not been carefully enough done: Pg 216 "'Show the Doctor to his, ah, quarters, then report the drive nacelle.'" Why? What has the drive nacelle done? Presumably this is supposed to be 'report to the drive nacelle.'
    5. Similarly: Pg 222 "The air was cold empty now that no one manned the ship's stations." A missing 'and' methinks.
    6. And again: Pg 248: "It was really the Rutan's plan that she was finding fault with, but she didn't have the nerve to tell it fact to... whatever,' Should be 'face to...' It wouldn't matter so much, but it's one half of a joke line.
    7. Pg 254 "'You've all still got all your fingers and toes or whatever?' 'As far as we can tell,' Turlough said, with a pointed glance at Fred. 'Good. Cross the lot of them.' Ignorant of Turlough's baffled expression..." How can Turlough, student at an English boarding school for several years, not have heard the expression 'cross your fingers'? And even if he hadn't, did Tegan never say it? This is ludicrous.

    1. The Doctor doesn't know much about dancing. He will learn later in the short story 'Teach Yourself Ballroom Dancing' in Short Trips: The Muses, but he's in his Sixth incarnation by then.
    2. It's possible that they have no visual records of the Sixth Doctor sections from The Two Doctors, given that all the Sontaran/Sixth Doctor interaction in that story takes place in Spain, not on Dastari's space station.
    3. Presumably there is some complicated code going on here - it's not a mathematical formula, but a combination of numbers and a word, designed to confuse the enemy.
    4. This is the Doctor listening and the TARDIS translation circuits hic-cup for a moment.
    5. Jahangir is stressed and not thinking with all his conjunctions.
    6. Maybe Nur is thinking that she needs to present certain facts to the Rutan and gets herself muddled.
    7. Turlough considered himself above such things, or he has heard of it, but Nur puts it in a strange way and he's concentrating on other things at the moment.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     There's nothing intrinsically wrong with this book. At first glance it has all the necessaries: space battles, an old enemy, complex and hard sci-fi. But somehow, something goes wrong. Maybe it's the writing style but somehow it's like you're watching the story through layers of tissue paper; it's all happening, but you can't bring yourself to care about anyone. There's no risk, no danger; everything just occurs, and we watch it do so. It feels like a poor episode of Star Trek (and the references to nacelles and captains 'engaging' their ships helps not at all to diffuse this sensation). True, the characterization of the Doctor is OK, but Turlough's all over the place. Having him occasionally think 'Oh gosh, I'm acting out of character' is not a good enough way around the problem, however. The notion of a colony based on India is nice, but even that is mostly thrown away. McIntee has proved that he can write, as in Sanctuary and The Eleventh Tiger, but on this occasion there's just no sparkle. I don't know. It's as simple as this: whenever I put it down, I had to really convince myself to pick it up again.
