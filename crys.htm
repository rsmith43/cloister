The Crystal Bucephalus
by Craig Hinton

Publisher: Virgin
ISBN: 0 426 20429 8

     

    BASIC PLOT
     Kidnapped whilst dining in Eighteenth-Century France, The Doctor, Tegan and Turlough find themselves thrust into a series of exciting exploits centering around the Bucephalus, a time-travelling restaurant. They become involved with a selection of characters all of whom appear to be related to one another, and a religious cult which has a somewhat flawed saviour. Oh, and the Doctor takes five years out to start up as a restaurantier.

    DOCTOR
     Fifth.

    COMPANIONS
     Tegan, Turlough and Kamelion

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 19 Tegan sees Earth Reptiles (and again in a painting on page 162), but fails to recognize them later in Warriors of the Deep.
    2. Pgs 25-26 "Don't patronize me, Doctor. I'm not one of those Earthlings that you usually surround yourself with. Or are you forgetting just who populated my planet in the first place?" It should be noted that, at this point, the Doctor has no idea where Turlough originally comes from, and will not find out until Planet of Fire.
    3. On Pg 52, much is made of the fact that Turlough doesn't have an actuator rod, thus meaning he has no connection back to the Bucephalus. Surely, when the Doctor and companions arrived at the Bucephalus, their total lack of any actuator rods would have cleared them of suspicion.
    4. Pg 69 dates The Crystal Bucephalus to later than 10,633AD. Which is all very well, but that's not 6 or 7 centuries into the tenth millennium as stated on Pg 5, but 6 or 7 centuries into the eleventh.
    5. Pg 142 "That bumbling pair of alcoholic Earth Reptiles had theorized that it took twenty minutes of absolute, Blinovitch, time for alterations in history to become permanent." That's a suspiciously round number! And I'm not sure it fits with Inferno, or indeed any other alternate universe stories that we've ever experienced.
    6. Pg 236 The Cloister Bell ringing buggers up Timewyrm: Genesys, which says it hasn't rung since the Logopolis affair. To be fair, the total collapse of the TARDIS is a good enough reason for it to ring.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. Tegan sees so many alien races in her travels with the Doctor that she doesn't necessarily remember the details, and they are never identified to her here. When it comes to aliens, she's better with names than faces.
    2. Turlough's being stroppy and perhaps says something that he didn't mean to say. Presumably the Doctor gave him a blank look and let it pass.
    3. No one thought to check whether they had actuator rods.
    4. The Doctor is falling into the trap that so many people make about centuries and things. It's just a slip of the tongue.
    5. When Gallifreyan technology isn't involved, this may well be the case, as every other occasion we've seen of massive Timeline alteration has involved something Gallifreyan, be it the TARDIS or the Doctor's Heart. Or, alternatively, the two alcoholic Earth Reptiles were wrong.
    6. The Doctor is not present on the TARDIS at this time, so has no recollection of the bell ringing later on. The Cloister Bell also rings in Enlightenment (amongst others) so the mistake really belongs to Timewyrm: Genesys.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     This is the book that made me give up reading the MAs first time around. As I was preparing to read it again, having hated GodEngine, I was dreading it, but I was somewhat surprised. True, there are many faults - Turlough practically vanishes midway through (even in scenes in which he is clearly present), the Doctor has to be surgically removed from the plot not once but twice, otherwise he'd've sorted it, and much of the dialogue, particularly in the first half of the book, has everyone being sarcastic just for the sake of it, every character acting as a Mary-Sue at some point. That said, the pace is cracking, interesting things keep happening, and the setting and scale of the book is magnificent. I'm not sure that it would give much reward on a reread (lots of corridor running-around and technobabble sorts all the problems out), but it's entertaining and creative, and certainly a fairly fun-filled way to spend a couple of hours. Better than you might expect.
