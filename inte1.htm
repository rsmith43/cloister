Interference Book 1: Shock Tactic
by Lawrence Miles

Publisher: BBC

    BASIC PLOT
     A race called the Remote are peddling something unfathomable called the Cold, but no one's sure why, least of all themselves. Alan Llewis thinks he's bought it, but he may well have got far more than he's bargained for. The Doctor's in a Saudi prison and Fitz went to bed and woke up six hundred years too late. That means it's left to Sam, ready to leave the TARDIS, and Sarah Jane, who already has, to save the world. Excellent. Meanwhile, the Third Doctor's walked into someone else's story: he's on Dust but really isn't supposed to be. And something bad is coming there and not even I.M. Foreman knows the answer. But keep watching, because, it turns out, the solution's almost certainly locked away somewhere in what we watch on TV. Somewhere in the signals.

    DOCTOR
     Eighth and Third Doctors, in that order.

    COMPANIONS
     With the Eighth Doctor: Sam Jones, Fitz Kreiner. Sarah Jane Smith is in both parts of the book, but in her 50s now in the Eighth Doctor. And K9, of course. We also meet Laura 'Compassion' Tobin for the first time, but she's not a companion yet.

    With the Third Doctor: Sarah Jane Smith

    CONTINUITY REFERENCES
     We've worked on the principle that no one in their right mind would only read one of the two Interference books at once, so we haven't mentioned all the continuity between the two books. Otherwise we'd be here until the end of time.

    Pg 1 "I.M. Foreman was sitting on the grass at the top of the hill." I.M. Foreman (as if you didn't know) allegedly owned the junkyard in which the whole thing began, all those distant years ago.

    Pg 3 "It's a universe-in-a-bottle" Goddess, where do we begin? This is the principle behind Dead Romance and the net cause of most of the problems in The Ancestor Cell, although no one saw that coming. The 'bottle' theory, in which the NAs and the BBC novels happened at different levels of reality, and the TV programme again (we infer) made such a terrible mess of continuity that unsnarling it's a nightmare. Even Lawrence Miles gets it wrong, maybe, as we'll see later. Suffice it to say that if you haven't dealt with the 'bottle problem', you must be a new-timer.

    "The way I was before. Only shorter." The Seventh Doctor. The Eighth is possibly watching one of the NAs happening, as - it's implied but never made clear - the NAs occurred in the Inner 'Virgin' bottle, but not in the outer 'BBC' one. Maybe.

    "I think the Time Lords are after some kind of escape route." Dead Romance.

    Pg 4 "Not that I should be getting involved in future events anyway." Consistent with the Doctor's approach in Alien Bodies.

    Pg 5 "Typical Time Lord. Always trying to fiddle with the props." Sounds like the process of filming the programme, in a way. Which, in a way, is what this book is all about.

    Pg 6 "'Sam was a good friend of mine,' he explained. 'Someone who helped me a lot, after my last regeneration.'" The Eight Doctors, although it's a bit of a bumping up of her part in that novel, given that she only appeared at the very beginning and the very end of it.

    "No. It's a bit of coincidence, though. Fitz was talking about The Lord of the Rings the first time I... well, never mind. Not important." 'Met him' are the missing words, and this was in The Taint, but of course.

    Pg 7 "Sam was a schoolgirl from London. That was what she was supposed to be, anyway. You remember London, don't you?" The Eight Doctors, and a reference to the I.M. Foreman junkyard in An Unearthly Child.

    "'Her timeline was altered,' the Doctor went on. 'Adjusted. By some very bad people.'" Alien Bodies and Unnatural History. The very bad people are Faction Paradox. Maybe...

    "They shaped her into the perfect travelling companion." A line from Alien Bodies.

    "Or maybe I was playing games with her timeline, and they were just the ones who made me realise it." Unnatural History again.

    "This isn't going to be one of those stories where everybody meets versions of themselves from parallel universes, is it?" Inferno, obviously, but possibly also a slightly snide reference to Unnatural History.

    "Only I've had enough of that kind of thing recently. What with the bottle and everything." Dead Romance, and the bottle theory again.

Llewis bears a startling resemblance in character type to Quixotl in Alien Bodies, at least at first. Also, this is an arms fair, not dissimilar to the auction scenario of the earlier Miles work.

Brian Blessed appeared in The Trial of a Time Lord, episodes 5-8 (Mindwarp). [Worryingly, the screaming continues.]" The creation of the Eye of Harmony, first heard of in The Deadly Assassin, transplanted without explanation to the Doctor's TARDIS in the Telemovie and mentioned in more books that I have hairs on my head. The screaming is, naturally, all the nasty things from outside the Universe trying to get in, which turn out to be the Vampires from State of Decay. Naturally, the purpose of this pair of books is to play with exactly that idea, given what happens at the end of part II.

That the 'dragonfly tattoo' is the mark of a Time Lord criminal was first mentioned in Christmas on a Rational Planet.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 33 "Sam was going to leave. Sam wanted to leave. Sam had decided to leave. Not one of those slow, creeping realisations, but an actual big, hard, no backing-out decision." But absolutely no mention made whatsoever of the reason for it that was given in Autumn Mist. Why not?
    2. Pg 41 "Padded suit, dyed-blond military haircut, dinky little UNISYC insignia on the shirt pocket." Back in Alien Bodies, UNISYC were described as being formed from the ashes of UNIT. But we know from Dominion that UNIT are alive and well three years later than this story is set. So what's going on?
    3. Pg 90 "MARK: It's 'cos your parents are total do-gooders. They won't even let their kid drink Coke." Not what we've been told. In Longest Day, Sam made that decision herself after she tried drugs once, had a bad experience, and took herself off everything that even vaguely affects the way the mind works.
    4. Pg 175 "'You're a political activist?' [...] 'I'm on Amnesty International's mailing list.'" According to Unnatural History, she was also head of her school's Amnesty Chapter; you'd have thought she might have mentioned this as well, in a conversation about doing something with your principles rather than just reading literature and saying how bad the world is.
    5. Pg 232 "That was what Rassilon had done to his people, when the Imprimiture had been worked into the biodata of the Time Lord elite." Perhaps that's related to the Rassilon Imprimature? (The spelling is confirmed by Robert Holmes himself, on page 107 of the novelisation of The Two Doctors.)
    6. Pg 264 "The Doctor stood there for a minute or so, watching the man sleep." Except that the man is the Eighth Doctor. In the past, the Doctor's different incarnations have always been able to recognize each other and, more to the point, these two have actually met before in The Eight Doctors. So why is the Third Doctor confused about who he is?

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. She made her decision then and is sticking by it, but, since it's been a little while and the TARDIS hasn't made it back to Earth in that time, she's mellowed about the reasons and now just wants to leave because it's time (as Mel once said) rather than because she's barely human anymore and fed up.
    2. UNISYC were created a long time before anyone knew they had been. They do appear to deal with the black ops, as it were.
    3. Sam's parents did indeed think that, but she ignored them, and then, based upon her experience, ended up doing what they said.
    4. Sam doesn't trust Compassion yet (and why should she?), so she's limiting the amount of information that she gives out.
    5. The Records of Rassilon are obviously hazy on the exact spelling.
    6. Let's face it, we've all been trying to forget the Eight Doctors. Maybe the Third Doctor is so embarrassed by what he tried to do then that he's edited it out of his mind (as page 262 implies), or maybe the Time Lords did it for him, understanding how he might feel when he thought about it afterwards. Meanwhile, because this is - as the Eighth Doctor says - a change in the established timeline, perhaps the lack of automatic recognition is a result of that.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Brilliant in some ways, criminally negligent in others. The Eighth Doctor appears in the grand total of three chapters, all of them while he's in prison. We don't even get to see him captured, which isn't the point, but it would have been nice to see him do something. But the book is just so well-written, cheerfully charming and deeply dark, that we're pretty much prepared to forgive it. OK, so the point it's trying to make (the nature of a society dependent on its television signals) is hammered home again and again, but at least it has a point to make and isn't just the silly run-around that so many other EDAs have been thus far. And the conversations and relationships between the characters are frequently absolutely fascinating. It's not completely perfect: very little actually happens for one thing, whilst Miles' contempt for the character of the Eighth Doctor comes through loud and clear, right from his very first appearance on the page, which is a shame. But, as a novel, it's thinking, and we can't fault it for that.
