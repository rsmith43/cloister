Dreamstone Moon
by Paul Leonard

Publisher: BBC
ISBN: 0 563 40585 6

     

    BASIC PLOT
     The Doctor traces Sam to the moon of Mu Camelopides VI, source of the Dreamstones, which are all the rage across the star systems. But the moon, and the Dreamstones are not what they seem. As the world begins to collapse around them, the Doctor realizes what the problem is. But no one is listening to him.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 22 "He'd heard of such things happening, since the Dalek war and the subsequent weakening of Earth's control in this sector". As far as we can tell from Legacy of the Daleks, Earth's barely got power enough to heat bathwater, let alone keep up an intergalactic fleet of ships. It's Legacy's fault, as everything else set in or near this time-zone is closer in technology levels to the description given here than that of Legacy, but it's still annoying.
    2. Pg 72 "Most of them were dreamies - dreamstone users who were incapable of creating their own dreams. They regarded anyone who could as a sort of minor god." It's a bit of a general point about the whole book, really, but why is there a need for dreamstones? To the best of my understanding, dreams happen to everyone, a way that the brain has of sorting through the detritus of the mind and packing bits away in the right places again. All right, you only remember your dreams if you wake up during them, but most people do that once in a while. So, why, suddenly are people not dreaming at all? Isn't it dangerous in some way to have no REM sleep? I wouldn't mind, but it's such an intrinsic plot-point, you'd've thought there would be some sort of explanation for this sudden dream famine that appears to be stalking the galaxy.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. We may have to assume that 'Earth' space control isn't actually on Earth at all but is, in fact, somewhere else in the Solar System. This kind of squares with Fear Itself, set in almost the same time period, although there's actually nothing in the text of any of these books to back up the assumption I'm making.
    2. Most space-faring races have also got the technology to mess with the way the brain works; it's true of us now, so there's no reason it shouldn't be true of us for the next few hundred years, or that other alien races wouldn't have comparative technology. Perhaps we and others used drugs to eliminate the need for REM sleep, perhaps in a desire to make us sleep less and become more productive, but forgot that this would eliminate dreams as well. It's a bit desperate, but I'm working with absolutely no clues at all here.

    FEATURED ALIEN RACES
     It's a Paul Leonard novel, so there are loads of them out there, all painstakingly created and never seen again after this novel.

    Krakenites: Massive swamp-dwelling creatures with one large eye, many tentacles and three legs.

    Zmm-Zmm: Insectoids with wings and antennae. If you insult them, you have to forfeit a pint of blood every week to feed their young.

    Jono is a Besiddian, a cat-like humanoid creature. It's doubtful that she's of the same species as the Sisters of Plenitude from New Earth, but it's possible I suppose. It would appear that Besiddians have a very high alcohol tolerance as she's had four or five bowls of Creme de Menthe. Male Besiddians are 2 1/2 metres tall, twice as heavy as the females and half as intelligent.

    Kram is a large spider, but it's not clear whether or not he's of the same species as the...

    Arachnons, spider-like beings who are eaten by the females of their species after mating, which is, to put it mildly, unfortunate. That said, they're quite blase about it.

    Chorodorons: white and six-limbed.

    Esqueekeemians: tiny, metal-winged tour-guides with a silly name. (Seriously, planet names and race names, when named by the race themselves, should be short and to the point. We're called, essentially, 'man' and live on a planet called, essentially 'mud'. Still, the new series has given us Raxacoricofallapatorius, so I suppose I shouldn't complain too much.)

    An Ixcallex is a millipede/caterpillar-type creature with a grey, segmented carapace. They use peroxide propellants.

    Vorsedds have horns, although it would appear that the females don't. They also have spines instead of hair and cheeks flushed red.

    Also mentioned are Borlogs and Geomydes.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     It's got a generally nice, if sometimes rather abrupt, writing style, although perhaps a few too many chapters have a tendency to trail off into sentence fragments as unconsciousness descends upon whichever character is unfortunate enough to appear at the end of said chapter. The Doctor, unusually for an early EDA, is wonderfully characterised, particularly when sad or angry (his line on page 200 is marvellous!), but the star of the show often turns out to be Aloisse, who sometimes comes across as more grown-up than the Doctor himself, let alone Sam. The ending, where the Doctor kills Anton by calming him as if he were a frightened child and telling him that it's 'time to sleep' manages to be both hugely anticlimactic and totally fitting after all the space battles that we've already had. It's a strange book in that I much preferred the second read, knowing already what was going to happen, which is not something that often occurs. Even Sam's bearable overall, and at least appears to be learning things. Best of all is the fascinating world-building and the chilling presentation of humanity in the wake of the Dalek invasion. Not perfect, but certainly not bad.
