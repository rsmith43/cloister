Mission: Impractical
by David A McIntee

Publisher: BBC
ISBN: 0 563 40592 9

     

    BASIC PLOT
     A missing artifact, stolen ten years ago, is about to provoke a war that will decimate the population of Vandor Prime. The Doctor and Frobisher are persuaded to help, and so re-assemble the team that originally stole it. Whilst trying to pull off the crime of the century, the Doctor is also aware that two highly paid and highly competent assassins are on his tail, put there by a shadowy figure with a grudge. And then there are the Ogrons...

    DOCTOR
     Sixth.

    The Valeyard is also present as a dark, shadowy, sinister presence. As far as we can tell.

    COMPANIONS
     Frobisher, the shape-shifting Whifferdill from the comic strips and the audios The Holy Terror and The Maltese Penguin.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 1 I'm not normally too bothered about contradicting the comics, but since this book actually includes a comics character... McIntee states in his Author's note that "This story takes place between the Marvel Doctor Who Magazine comic strips War Game and Fun House." Except that it's really difficult to make that fit. Certainly War Game and Fun House don't have Peri in, but later adventures with the Doctor and Frobisher do, and she's picked up from Twentieth Century Earth (in Kane's Story), where she clearly isn't by the end of Mindwarp, so the Doctor must have left her there for a time before picking her up again for the run of comics and then, after dropping Frobisher off somewhere, eventually, the events of The Trial of a Time Lord take place. However, Mission: Impractical clearly takes place after The Trial of a Time Lord (the Doctor refers to Trial in dialogue), so when, exactly, does it happen?
    2. On Pg 33 the Doctor states "There. They won't find it so easy to follow us [...] In fact I imagine they'll find it impossible; I've set the TARDIS to make a series of random jumps." And then, on Pg 34, the Doctor sets the TARDIS to go to the Gamma Dephinus system, approximately (allowing for speed of dialogue) 45 seconds later. So which is it, Doctor?
    3. Pgs 136 "'I killed the prosecutor.' Glitz was suddenly disappointed. [...] You didn't get off that way, they'd just draft in another prosecutor." Except that Glitz was there for the last two episodes of Trial of a Time Lord, so it's odd that he seems surprised by this. Possibly he didn't know the outcome... but on page 140 he also appears surprised that the Doctor was tried for genocide, despite having been an actual witness at his trial.
    4. Pg 140 "The Doctor paused. 'Probably because those things were are all true.'" I beg your pardon?
    5. Pg 195 "The Doctor had known better than to prohibit the ue of guns." He probably prohibited their use also.
    6. Pg 250 "Probably the same someone who supplied that ratty little scientist with the details on how to make vraxoin. The secret's been lost for centuries, but a Time Lord could always pop back and collect it." Actually, if we assume it's the Valeyard that is behind this drugs scam, he probably just remembers it from Nightmare of Eden. That said, however the information has been brought into the new 'present day', an earlier analysis of the vraxoin (on page 159) states that this batch wasn't made from Mandrells, so it would appear that no one has popped back to find the secret, as this vraxoin was made in a completely different way.
    7. Pg 255 "Dibber rolled over, and Glitz saw the cauterised hole that was burned through his back. His eyes were still open, but Dibber wasn't seeing anything with them." Dibber's death would have all made perfect sense, given his absence in Dragonfire, were it not for the fact that he is present in Goth Opera, which, given that it is set on the Nosferatu II, is clearly set later.
    8. Pgs 257-258 "'No response. There is no electrochemical or EM activity in the brain or nervous system.' There was a long indrawn breath. 'At last...' Frobisher could almost hear the satisfied smile." The Valeyard (we assume) is undoubtedly pleased when the Doctor dies, but it's hard to see exactly why, or what he was trying to achieve here. If the deal with the High Council still stands (albeit that this is unlikely, given that said High Council has been impeached by this point), then he should inherit the Doctor's remaining lives; since the Doctor isn't, in fact, dead, he presumably doesn't. So doesn't he notice that fact? And if that deal doesn't stand, surely the Doctor's random death will just ensure that the Valeyard never comes into existence anyway, given that he isn't really created until somewhere between the Doctor's twelfth and final incarnations? The Valeyard always was something of a nonsense, but this is even moreso.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. There are two possible answers. The first is that, despite the order in which they were produced, War Game and Fun House actually occurred way later in the Doctor's time-line than it would originally appear. The second is that, since it's only in the Author's Note, we can safely ignore McIntee's placing, and just assume (as has generally been done) that the Doctor picks Frobisher up again sometime after The Trial of a Time Lord, having dropped him off some time before that story when he was still travelling with Peri. It's annoying though, because it wouldn't be a problem if McIntee hadn't tried to fit it in the chronology.

    3. The Time Lords, as they are wont to do, erased much of Glitz's memory of the events of the trial, so his memories of various events are sketchy.
    4. The Doctor is clearly putting an oblique in his sentence ('those things were/are all true') and Glitz, who didn't study hard enough at reform school, isn't grammatically aware enough to hear it in that way.

Now, with modern technology, a form of the drug can be created without use of that strangely comedic and now sadly extinct species.
    7. As suggested on the Goth Opera page, Glitz missed Dibber so much that, sometime after Dragonfire, he had him cloned.
    8. The Valeyard is currently occupying the body of the Keeper of the Matrix, one assumes, so the death of the Doctor now has no effect on him. We can assume that the Valeyard is working for or with the CIA, so perhaps the deal is with them rather than the old High Council. Something must change after this time, however, as, by Matrix, the Valeyard is not trying to kill the Doctor so much as steal his remaining lives again. We can therefore assume the following: The Valeyard is in the Keeper's body in much the same way that the Master inhabits Tremas's form at the end of The Keeper of Traken. In this story then, with the Doctor dead, the Valeyard is set to inherit his remaining lives (the Doctor even signed the form and everything back in Trial of a Time Lord) and they would be added to the 'Keeper's' regenerations. However, when it turns out the Doctor is not even vaguely dead, the Valeyard does not inherit any such thing and, as the body of the Keeper slowly disintegrates, he has to change his plans, hence his actions in Matrix. You have to work damn hard, however, to make that all fit together, and something more of the Valeyard's motivation or reasoning would have been nice. Alternatively, and I'm going out on a limb here, despite all the assumptions made and, possibly, McIntee's intentions, Mr Zimmerman isn't the Valeyard at all. It could be a random CIA agent. It could even be the Master, for all we know.

     The Veltrochni, the younger of which have whiter quills than the elder. Their ships are designated Dragons or Mage-Dragons. The Veltrochni also appeared in The Dark Path and are mentioned in Lords of the Storm.

    Sha'ol is the last of the S'Raph Tzun, who have previously appeared in First Frontier and Bullet Time. They, also, are mentioned in Lords of the Storm. They are short and grey are, to all intents and purposes, the 'greys' that the X-Files made famous. Their home planet was called S'Arl, before it was destroyed by the Veltrochni. At least some Tzun, including Sha'ol, have genetically enhanced memories, although this would seem only to be (some of) the pure-breed S'Raphs. At least one other branch of Tzun appear to exist, the Ph'Sor Tzun, but they are half-breeds.

    Ogrons, many and varied, although all the ones we meet here end up dead. The race first appeared in Day of the Daleks. Their home planet is called Orestes, although the Orgons refer to it as Braah. This is actually a little racist on behalf of the rest of the Universe - the Ogrons might be stupid, but you should still do them the courtesy, surely, of referring to their planet by the name they give it.     IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     All right, I've been busy, but it took me weeks to get through this. An author should never suggest that you won't need your brain in order to read his own book, but rarely has such warning been so true. Yes, all right, the Robert Holmes style is there in abundance, in Glitz (spot-on characterization) and Dibber (a 'classic Holmsian double act' if ever I saw one), in the general style and in the many references to Holmes stories. And yet, it's not Robert Holmes, and it suffers hugely from trying to be. It's also not the Blues Brothers, which it also desperately wishes to homage. And I didn't think it was possible to make Ogrons this unfunny. We spend stupid amounts of time in a build-up to a heist that takes approximately four pages - a massive imbalance in plotting that is quite unforgiveable - while the lack of any form of resolution or explanation of the Mr Zimmerman plot thread is intensely annoying. It picks up at the end, roughly at the point where the Ogron song appears, as people get their just deserts, the Veltrochni prove how much they have learned, and the whole thing's both quite uplifting, and nicely sad (particularly Kala's reaction to her husband's actions). But you've got to wade through treacle to get that far, and, by the time you do, you have to really work hard to care about it.
