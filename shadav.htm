    BASIC PLOT
     With his TARDIS destroyed, the Doctor is lost in Avalon, the land of human dreams. The Brigadier is here too, but soon the old friends will find themselves on opposite sides of the same conflict. And there are other forces at play here, with designs on a different future, and the part that Compassion will play in it.

    DOCTOR
     Eighth.

    COMPANIONS
     Fitz and Compassion, with the Brigadier and Romana III, although the latter's status as a companion is somewhat dubious by now.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 14 The name Cavisadoratrelundar appears to suggest a connection, somehow, to Romana, but it should be noted that Romana's full name has an extra 'v' that somehow Cavis has misplaced: it's Romanadvoratrelundar. Now, this might be deliberate, but it seems more likely to be a failing of memory on Cornell's part.
    2. Pg 15 "She wandered to the Time/Space Visualiser" In The Chase, this is called the "Time and Space visualiser" (it's muffled, but Hartnell definitely says "and").
    3. Pg 16 "Cavis made the Horns of Rassilon in the direction of the elevator." When this was first mentioned, in Timewyrm: Revelation, it was hilarious, as the Doctor raised two fingers in an ancient Gallifreyan protective gesture - the Horns of Rassilon - which turned out to be quite rude on Earth. Its use now by Cavis to mean, well, pretty much exactly what it does mean on Earth, is something of a shame, as well as contradicting what we learned about the gesture the first time.
    4. Pg 56 "This world's as big as the whole Isle of Greater Pryddein." A corruption, obviously, of Great Britain, and one that is used in Arthurian legend and modern rewrites thereof. However, in every single source, it's spelled 'Prydein'.
    5. Pg 87 "The Royal Corps of Engineers had laid metal trackways down the chalk path he'd walked it seemed an eternity before, and, to the amazement of the locals, main battle tanks and other pieces of hardward regularly trundled up to the station in the early hours, the drivers as baffled as anybody else as to what they were going to do at such a small facility." Okay, so this sentence is not technically a cock-up. But it should be.
    6. Pg 104 "And, for some reason that alluded him, he started to laugh." Eluded, surely?
    7. Pg 119 "His beery gaze had swept up to take in the curious constellations glittering above, a perfect replica of the stars seen from Earth." The Doctor said earlier that they match those of Earth 2012 (Pg 35), and the Brigadier's stated that they're ice crystals stuck to the vault (Pg 109). Have they moved over the last 2000 years consistently and continously to match the ones on the original Earth? This seems unlikely in the extreme.
    8. Pg 146 "Central, eh? So what's Goth been telling you about me?" Er, Goth's dead. And the Doctor knows that, since he was the one who killed him. Even Terrance Dicks knew that in The Eight Doctors. So what's going on?
    9. Pg 252 "And then she smiled broadly at the sight of Compassion. 'Type 102'" Way back in The Dimension Riders, the President had a Type 102 TARDIS, and it wasn't Compassion. The very nature of Compassion ensures that she's unique. Lawrence Miles obviously followed the numbering from The Dimension Riders, but didn't account for the gap that would come before. Cornell, presumably, hoped that no one would notice...
    10. Pg 253 "We'll take you back to Gallifrey and mate you with another capsule." Without meaning to appear indelicate, we have to ask: how, precisely? Compassion is unique. Until this point, there were no female or male TARDISes in any shape or form, so no other 'capsule' would have the necessary, erm, accoutrements.

    2. It's known by several names.
    3. It's shifted in meaning. Unfortunate though, and somehow lazy.
    4. Blame the Brothers Grimm - it's bound to do with consonantal shift.
    5. Wasn't there an editor on hand who could streamline this sort of thing? Oh wait...
    6. Editing has clearly alluded this book.
    7. But it must be the case. It transpires that the constellations are indeed continually updated as according to Constantine's connection with Rex. What an attention to detail the sleeping king has.
    8. It's just about possible that the Doctor's still doing what he was doing on the previous page by saying the wrong thing to get Cavis and Gandar to blurt out what the truth is.     IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Still better than so many of the books around it, Avalon is equally not Cornell at his best. The land of Avalon itself is horribly contrived; even Fitz thinks so (page 133) and, as always, when your characters start talking to you as an author, you need to listen. Similarly, the plot and its resolution aren't great, Cavis and Gandar should somewhere have been stopped with extreme prejudice and what happens to Romana is unforgiveable. But... if you disregard what the story is about, and half the characters, the actual thing of the book - the process of grief and self-forgiveness on the parts of both the Brigadier and, to a lesser extent, the Doctor - is quite fantastic. The justifications for the Doctor's behaviour and his acceptance of his own shortcomings in this incarnation are long overdue and received with deep gratitude. And, this being Cornell, the power, beauty and sheer simplicity of the writing when dealing with that grieving process is quite incredible (I challenge anyone to read pages 207-213 without getting at least a little teary-eyed). Sadly, though, in the end, Avalon requires a huge effort to read: barring the Brigadier talking to Doris, it just doesn't sing. Very worthy and all, but it just doesn't sing.
