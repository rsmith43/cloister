Sanctuary
by David A. McIntee

Publisher: Virgin
ISBN: 0 426 20439 5

     

    BASIC PLOT
     Escaping a natural disaster that affects the TARDIS, the Doctor and Benny end up stranded in Medieval France during the Albigensian Crusade. While the Doctor finds himself working with and against the oppressors, Benny meets Guy de Carnac, and falls in love. This can't possibly end well.

    DOCTOR
     Seventh.

    COMPANIONS
     Benny.

    MATERIALISATION CIRCUIT
     Pg 57 The Jade Pagoda materializes in a village North-West of Beziers, France, in 1242AD. There's something quite lovely about the fact that the materialization appears to sound like wind-chimes.

    Pg 230 The TARDIS proper replaces the Jade Pagoda in exactly the same place, just about a week later.

    Pg 294 The TARDIS rematerializes again in the same place, a few hours after it had left.

    NEW FRIENDS AND NEW ENEMIES
     It's a bloodbath. Practically everyone we meet dies during the course of the book or in the massacre that follows. Beyond a few nameless soldiers and a selection of Templar Knights, the only clear named survivor is Fransisco Guzman. And even in this case, the book tells us that, despite his desire to be the next Pope, he's never going to make it and is also not long for this world.

    That said, it is deliberately left unclear as to whether or not Guy de Carnac survives, although the implication is that he does not. McIntee was apparently at one point commissioned to write more stories about this character, but we cannot infer his survival as it remains unclear whether they were to be set before or after the events of this novel. These books (I believe there were to be three) have, to the best of my knowledge, yet to be either written or published.

She must have been quite considerably surprised, given that Edouard told her that they were friends about helf a page earlier.

When I was a child, a race called the Daleks laid siege to Vandor Prime, bombarding it night and day [...] They killed my mother as I watched." This is all true of course, except that it happened on Beta Caprisis, not Vandor Prime, which was the location for Mission: Impractical.
    5. Pg 205 "I should have joined the the Samaritans" Huh?
    6. Pg 216 "Then she'd still been fleeing the sting of betrayal when she'd found out the truth about Kyle." This is true, of course, except that it happened to Simon, not Kyle. Kyle was Benny's co-worker in Love and War, not lover.
    7. Pg 255 The murder mystery, sadly, collapses in upon itself: Jeanne: "I did not intend to kill Girard. I had hoped only to extract the hiding place of the skull from him, but he threw himself on to my dagger rather than tell me." However, on Pg 246, the Doctor calculates who the murderer is by working out how far they could have run between the time of Girard's scream and the time of Bernice's arrival at Girard's room. If Jeanne is to be believed, the 'murder' was an accident, which means that in the time that it took Bernice to get there, not only did she have to run the allotted distance, she also had to have time to think up the 'locked room' mystery, move the table and the poker into position, balance the door on her hurriedly constructed contraption and slam it hard. Going on all the available evidence, it just doesn't work.
    8. Pgs 265-266 "Regardless of where he went, there was nowhere that his presence - or the hunters it would drawn - would not put Bernice in more danger than he was willing to allow." Say what?
    9. Pg 272 "Still, it seemed that the sun was setting setting on Amor." This is, presumably, a misprint, although, with an additional comma, it would have been a lovely piece of writing.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. The inkeeper sees a lot that's unusual in his daily grind.
    2. Necros was, perhaps, originally discovered by Professor Nekros and the spelling changed over time. In Benny's time, it presumably has its original spelling.
    3. Perhaps she's just surprised that Guy has any friends in the first place.
    4. For reasons of her own, perhaps to distance herself mentally from the events, Benny is changing the name of the place. She does it a lot though, even in her own thought-processes, which is odd. It must be a traumatic side-effect, similar to her sticking post-it notes over facts in her diary.
    5. Benny isn't interested in joining the Samaritans, but rather the the Samaritans, an altogether more debauched group.
    6. Return of the Living Dad later retcons this, providing Benny's lover with the full name of Simon Kyle.
    7. Jeanne is lying through her teeth, and fully intended to kill Girard, with the plan completely in place. Or she runs much, much faster than the Doctor. Or, most likely, Girard died silently and it was her that screamed once she had set the room up.
    8. Guy is fierce with a paintbrush and can draw some majorly badass hunters.
    9. The sun was really serious about its setting that evening.

    FEATURED LOCATIONS
     The town of Beziers (which includes a pub called the Cockatrice, the church of Saint Jean and the Hotel de Ville, among other buildings) on the Southern end of the East Pyrenees in Southern France. All the events of the novel occur either in this town or in its environs. The Inquisition are holed up in Beziers itself, whilst the Parfaits and their followers and protectors are in a sanctuary on a mountain-top called The Roc. Various other villages and areas of open countryside in the neighbourhood are visited. The year is 1242AD, and the action takes place over a period of just over a week.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     McIntee proves that he is far better at action sequences involving swords than he is in other books when they involve spaceships. It's great book; the villains are marvellous: Guzman is the nastiest little hypocrite you're ever likely to meet. The quasi-Medieval dialogue is similar in style and sparkle to that of The Crusade and the whole thing feels just like the best Hartnell historicals, even down to the lack of access to the TARDIS and the way the Doctor and Benny are so spectacularly split up to experience the two different sides of the story. The writing as Benny falls in love and completely fails to notice it is quite lovely, and, as an abject contrast, the Doctor being forced to watch others being tortured is horrific but extremely powerful in the understanding we get of his powerlessness. It is interesting that Guy, whom Bernice falls in love with, is drawn like a Doctor from Medieval times, with the same guilt, the same heartfelt belief in making things better against all the odds, and the same desire to run into danger and death. The Doctor does little, basically just solving a simple 'locked room' mystery (and he even admits to the reader that it's simple) but that matters not, for this is Bernice's story, and it's beautifully played. Love and War lost Ace to the Doctor for sex; Sanctuary doesn't lose Bernice, but it's about love, and is all the more powerful for that.
