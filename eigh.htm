The Eight Doctors
by Terrance Dicks

Publisher: BBC
ISBN: 0 563 40563 5

     

    BASIC PLOT
     The Eighth Doctor succumbs to a trap left by the Master and loses his memories (how prophetic). The TARDIS takes him to meet all his former selves in order to regain them again. That's pretty much it.

    DOCTOR
     All of the first eight, numbers one through seven in sequence.

    COMPANIONS
     Sam Jones is introduced. She will remain with the Doctor until Interference II. The Gallifrey Chronicles implies that she died sometime thereafter. Still, you've got to laugh, haven't you?

    Ian, Susan and Barbara appear in cameo.

    Jamie and Zoe appear as part of The War Games (reprise)

    Jo Grant as part of The Sea Devils.

    Romana, in her second incarnation, appears immediately after State of Decay.

    Tegan and Turlough, and quite fun they are too.

    Mel, but only briefly and on the Matrix screen.

    PREPARATORY READING
     Everything's explained in nice simple language as we go along, but you might want to read Blood Harvest just to see how much continuity can endeavour to collapse in upon itself.

    Pg 4 "A word formed itself in his mind: amnesia." Now there's a word the Eighth Doctor is going to get increasingly familiar with. At least this time, it only takes him a book's worth to get his memory back. (Interestingly, the Doctor's amnesia is very similar to Ace's in Timewyrm: Genesys, in that he knows practical things, but nothing about himself. This is for the same reason plotwise as well, in that it introduces the uninitiated to the world of Who. No one expected it on this scale, however.)

    "He felt a girl's warm lips on his own and heard a voice shout exultantly, 'I am the Doctor!' The voice was his own." The Telemovie.

    "It called up a shadowy picture of a great vaulted chamber in which a shaft of light picked out a massive stone bier. On the top of the bier lay a motionless form, dressed in ancient ceremonial robes. A frieze of Time Lord images ran around the sides of the bier, but the eyes in the stone faces were furiously alive." This is the tomb of Rassilon, from The Five Doctors.

    Pg 6 "A faded sign was propped against one wall. TOTTERS LANE YARD, I.M. FOREMAN, PROP." This is accurate to An Unearthly Child, I think.

    "The place had been closed for years now - a junkyard that had been junked. It had a sinister reputation that went back over thirty years. Something about a mysteriously appearing and disappearing police box. There didn't seem to be any sign of it now, but there had been tales of people just disappearing - and about strange silvery monsters." The police box itself actually only appeared and disappeared once to the best of our knowledge (it did move around in Time and Relative, but no one saw this happen). The silver monsters refers to Attack of the Cybermen (when the Chameleon Circuit was functioning, so still no Police Box, and, for that matter, Cybermen never appeared in the junkyard (to the best of our knowledge)). Strange that there is no reference to the massive Dalek incursion here in 1963, although presumably that's because of the Official Secrets Act. (Note also that the Police Box didn't land at the junkyard in that story.) People disappearing - Ian and Barbara in this case - is accurate. Also see Interference, part II for more information that you could possibly want to know about the provenance of the junkyard on Totter's Lane.

    Pg 9 "We all know you're Coal Hill School's friendly neighbourhood dope dealer." Susan went to this school, where Ian and Barbara were teachers. It seems to have gone downhill since then.

This is the Doctor remembering his training in Venusian Aikido, which is alluded to in Venusian Lullaby and mentioned in the short story Venusian Sunset from the collection, Perfect Timing.

    Pg 12 "In fact he had become extremely adept at Venusian Aikido. Few two-armed lifeforms could claim as much." Venusians have five legs, as we see in Venusian Lullaby.

    Pg 14 "Were you aware that this stuff was being peddled in your area? There was nothing like that going on in Coal Hill when I used to live here." Time and Relative and An Unearthly Child. And see Continuity Cock-Ups.

    Pg 15 "Another dormant memory revived. 'Smith. Doctor John Smith.'" A pseudonym used by the Doctor since The Wheel in Space.

    Pg 19 "'What do you claim to be qualified in?' 'Practically everything.'" A claim initially made by the Second Doctor. Can't remember when though.

    "'How about giving us your real name?' 'Oh no, I couldn't do that,' said the Doctor, looking shocked. 'Why not?' 'It's secret. Confidential. They used to call me Theta Sigma at the Academy, but that was more of a nickname really.'" The Armageddon Factor. Scarily, this perpetually shocked and naive characterization of the Eighth Doctor is one which would continue in other 8DAs, leading to his nickname of 'the congenital idiot' by much of fandom.

    Pg 23 "'You know Marilyn Simms in Year Five?' 'The one with the outstanding - personality?' said Trev." It's obvious what Trev means. I mention this briefly, because by this point in most comprehensive schools in England, Year 5 means 9-10 year olds, not 15-16, which is, by 1997, referred to as Year 11. Which, if nothing else, makes Trev's comment somewhat alarming. Presumably Coal Hill's quite outdated.

Sorry, but it does seem an inexcusable shame to reinterpret in such a ham-fisted way.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS
     Not as many as I'd expected, but most of them are cosmically disastrous...

    1. Pg 14 "Were you aware that this stuff was being peddled in your area? There was nothing like that going on in Coal Hill when I used to live here." Except that at this point the Doctor remembers nothing about his life at all, so should have no recollection of living here in any way, shape or form.
    2. Pgs 36-37 "'Well, be off with you, before the time bubble bursts and the others see you.' 'Time bubble?' The Doctor looked at the group working on the stretcher and realized for the first time that they were frozen, motionless. 'State of temporal stasis - freak effect of crossing the time streams. It won't last forever.'" Why has this never happened before? Or since? It's clearly a plot contrivance.
    3. Pg 39 The nightmare that is the Flavia Presidency creates one of the most confusing situations in Who continuity. If you take it all in the order it appears to be in: Flavia becomes Acting President after The Five Doctors, Niroc replaces her (just before the Trial of a Time Lord period), after which Borusa takes over for a time and then, it would seem, Flavia again. She's certainly president by Blood Harvest, which is when Romana returns to Gallifrey (it happens in Goth Opera, but Gallifrey-wise, it's contemporary with Blood Harvest). Thereafter, Romana becomes President (she is by the time of Lungbarrow), but now, seemingly after that, Flavia is president yet again, and has been for some time. Then Romana will be President by the time of The Shadows of Avalon. And let's not even mention the audios, which have yet another President.
    4. Pg 42 Flavia lists the three occasions that the Doctor has crossed his own time-stream: The Three Doctors, The Five Doctors and The Two Doctors. Strangely, she fails to mention the fact that she has already experienced the Eighth Doctor crossing the time-stream of the Sixth despite the fact that she experiences it in about 150 pages in the past of her own time-stream. You could just about get away with it, but for the fact that without the Eighth Doctor interfering, she wouldn't be in the position of President and therefore able to make these comments. She also fails to mention Cold Fusion, although it's possible (particularly given the mess that the Vortex is in that story) that no one actually noticed.
    5. Pg 66 "And did you happen to hear what he what he said would happen if I did?" There's no excuse for that level of poor proof-reading.
    6. Pg 95 "'It's Colonel Lethbridge-Stewart, isn't it?' [...] 'I remember you did terribly well in that nasty business with the Intelligence - Yeti in the Underground and all that.'" Yes, but the Eighth Doctor now has his memories up to The War Games, which therefore must include The Invasion, in which the Brigadier has already been promoted to, well, Brigadier.
    7. Pg 106 "For a time the red segment [The Eighth Doctor's time track] continued to move towards the fourth blue one, then it disappeared altogether from the screen. 'Well?' snapped President Flavia. 'What does this mean?' 'I am not sure, Madam President,' said Volnar miserably. 'Is the Doctor dead?' 'It is possible, Madam President. Or perhaps he has somehow gone beyond the range of the temporal scanning equipment.'" We all know that the Doctor's gone to E-Space, but why has his whole track vanished? Surely everywhere he's been, he's still been to? More to the point, surely the Fourth Doctor's timetrack should also be missing, or at least have a segment vanished from it (Full Circle etc). And the Seventh, for that matter (Blood Harvest). It's a semi-artistic representation, I admit, but it still makes absolutely no internal sense whatsoever. (Incidentally, Blood Harvest makes it clear that K9 calculated a way into and out of E-Space whenever the Doctor wanted, so, although it may appear so, the Eighth Doctor's ability to get there is not a continuity error.)

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     This is Terrance Dicks at his least sophisticated. Let me say that again. This is Terrance Dicks at his least sophisticated. We all knew that all of Terrance Dicks' books were sequels to his television stories. What no one expected was that he'd try to do them all at once, including some that already had sequels that he himself had written. It's... well, it's pretty awful actually, with a writing style that talks down to the reader, abysmal characterization (check out the Coal Hill School teachers; actually, for that matter, check out Sam) and a plot which is paper thin. Normally, we'd forgive Terrance this, because generally in his books there are at least some nice touches and a charming self-awareness, but both these features are conspicuous by their absence here. It's almost as if he saw the Telemovie and thought, 'Well, if that's all they can be bothered to do, then this is all they deserve.' Which is a shame. Couple this with the clear bile he directs at some aspects of the show (Trial of a Time Lord, the NAs and the Telemovie particularly), appalling characterization of almost all the Doctors as well as Dicks' trademark sexism and you have an unsatisfying mess which, for a fan, leaves a nasty taste in the mouth while, for a newcomer, with its endless (bad) continuity, must have been incomprehensible at best. Not the best way to start a series of novels, really.

    Addendum: And who thought that letting Terrance create the new companion was a clever idea? Did no one realize that he'd just create Jo again, only with his own startlingly outdated ideas of 1990s youth culture? No wonder we ended up with Sam!
