by Daniel Blythe

Publisher: Virgin

    BASIC PLOT
     The last of the Sensopaths, a being which houses three personalities in one, is unleashed on the Universe. Beings of absolute power, they are trying to kick-start the Infinite Requiem, a process by which all minds in the Universe will meld in to one, apparently. From 1997 to the Twenty-Fifth Century to almost the end of time, the Doctor is there, determined to stop them.

    PREPARATORY READING
     It follows on from Set Piece but, despite occasional references to Ace's recent departure, there's no real need to have read it (other than the fact that it's really quite good).

    Elements of this story reappear from The Dimension Riders.

It's unclear which Cyberwars are being referred to here, since the Cyberwars that we know of are generally accepted to have occurred in the twenty-sixth century (this was a common error in the NAs). We must therefore assume that there is another sequence of Cyberwars about which we are unfamiliar, and that the 'colonial incidents' referred to are actually part of the expansion of the Earth Empire, and the problem with settling and holding planets, rather than the ones so often visited by the Third Doctor which tended to be about its contraction.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 67 "'It's imperative,' said Cheynor sternly. 'You both realize,' he added, leaning forwards with his hands folded under his dark beard, 'that if the Phractons were to launch a full-scale attack on this base, we would have little chance of repelling them?'" The really appalling punctuation in this section of dialogue means that, while the words would almost make sense if you were hearing them, they don't make any sense written down, as he appears to start making a statement and then ends it as a question.

While the Rani had one in The Mark of the Rani, and the Master built one in First Frontier, the Sixth Doctor claims in The Two Doctors that he always wanted one and, tellingly, doesn't actually own one. It's the Second Doctor that has one, and it's working fine.
    3. Pg 142 "It's a very basic thought wave damping device. I wish I'd had this when I met the Vardans." This is careless - it's in a cupboard that appears to have been untouched for ages, along with stuff from The Two Doctors and Black Orchid, all of which goes to imply that he did, in fact, have it when he met the Vardans in No Future.
    4. Pg 185 "'Clear as Draconian chess, Doctor,' said Liebniz." Except this is 2387, some hundred or so years before the first encounter between Humans and Draconians, as made clear in Frontier in Space. So to whom is Liebniz referring? (Later, on Pg 249, it transpires that Liebniz's wife was killed using a Draconian toxin as well.)
    5. Pg 196 The Phractron Commandant cannot fit through the TARDIS doors, and the Doctor has to materialise around him, despite later the Doctor being able to drive a VW Bug through said doors (Vampire Science etc) and getting an entire herd of somewhat wide Skarasen through them in The Bodysnatchers.     6. Pg 225 "She remembered the time that an all too convincing virtuality of Heaven, her home-world, had been set up inside the TARDIS." Except we all know that Heaven's just the place that Bernice met the Doctor. She was born on Beta Caprisis.

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. Cheynor starts to say one thing ('It's imperative,') then changes his mind about how he's going to phrase it, interrupts himself, starts again and re-phrases his thought processes into a question rather than a statement. As you do.
    2. It never made sense in The Two Doctors either: how can the Second Doctor own something that the Sixth Doctor had always wanted? So: World Game clearly states that the Second Doctor in The Two Doctors is working for the Time Lords post-The War Games, and they gave him the Stattenheim. Thereafter, when he was recalled to Gallifrey before his exile actually did begin, and he regenerated, he hid it deep in the TARDIS, and then had its location mind-wiped when his memories of Season 6b were removed. The Seventh Doctor later found it (either by accident or the mind-blocks collapsed when he regenerated for the sixth time) but, after hundreds of years, it was broken. He has since repaired it.
    3. He's actually referring here to when he met the Vardans in The Invasion of Time. He did have it available in No Future had he desired, but, fearful of the lack of the notes that he normally left for himself in that story (the Monk had stolen them all, you'll recall) he decided not to use it on the off-chance that his future self would attempt to contact him or one of his companions by telepathy.
    4. As suggested on the page for To The Slaughter, the Draconians mentioned here are, in fact, a group of Amish-like breakaway humans. As well as living their own lives, separate from anyone else, they also developed a particularly fiendish and difficult version of normal chess, which, despite their best efforts, spread across the universe and became popular for a time. They also amused themselves by creating new and ever-more deadly toxins in order to kill everyone else in the whole universe, just so they could be more alone. Nice bunch.
    5. The Doctor was so embarrassed about his social faux-pas on this occasion that he later adjusted the dimensional configuration of the doors so that such an unfortunate incident would never happen again.
    6. Benny's been travelling so much with the Doctor by this point that she sometimes thinks of Heaven as her home world, given that it was the last regular home she had.

    FEATURED ALIEN RACES
     The Sensopaths, the most powerful telepaths the Doctor has ever encountered, who appear to be a composite being made up of three individuals who are one, although they appear to be able to develop or have different moralities and plans. In looks they are extremely tall (roughly two metres) with large faces and wide, almost clown-like mouths (in a disturbing kind of way). They have an incredible amount of power over both the mental and physical worlds. They do not understand the concept of guilt. Despite being scattered across time, they are able to communicate with each other. They are the last of their race and 'kill' themselves at the end of the book, although there is a hint that one, at least, remains behind in the mind of Horst.

    Phractons - a kind of composite-minded creature, although with each mind being an individual in and of itself. Collectively, they are known as a Swarm. They are part-organic, part-cybernetic, but are separate beings who travel around in highly-armed globes, which fire a combustible gas and then ignite it (rather like the Mechanoids appear to have done). They are individuals and have different levels of morality depending on which one you are experiencing. That said, most of them seem quite vicious and it's a bit of a wonder that the Commandant, given his comparative pacifism, could have risen to be in charge when the rest of them seem to be gun-toting mass murderers.

    The Pridka, a race of telepathic beings from the far future, with 36 senses. They have a crest of fins which can change colour, while their skin is basically blue. They tend to be either mostly male or mostly female, with aspects of each gender. They have at least four stomachs. The Pridka would appear to be a race with an incredibly low population, as Amarill's surname includes the race name, which is akin to calling me Anthony Wilson Human.

    Monoids and Rills.

    Rakkhins, a race of telepaths who need to be constantly immersed in a supersaturated solution of ammonia.

    The Yzashoks, a race of philosophers who spend their time considering the confluence between the harmony of the self and the communion with others. They exist in an amniotic fluid attuned to their brain patterns.

    The Borsii, who have tiny bodies and massive wedge-shaped heads. They appear to exist purely to faciliate a joke about the race getting big-headed.

    FEATURED LOCATIONS
     The planet of Gadrell Major, in the area of space known as Gadrell Major Dominion, Common Era 2387. The main city we visit on the planet is called Banksburgh, a city set out in the Wheel of Life pattern, and which has areas including Londinium Plaza, Corporation Boulevard, Argolis Avenue and Dororvernum Square. It has a Library and an ice rink. We also visit the outskirts of the city. Other areas on Gadrell Major include the McNab Gorges and Kaneston (which, by the by, if it were spelled 'Caneston' would be a medicine used to treat Thrush in 21st century England!)

    Some of the action in the 2387 time-zone takes place on the Phoenix, a Spacefleet vessel commanded by Cheynor. Towards the end of the book, the Darwin also turns up.

    A city in England, Earth, 1997. For some reason (unless I've missed it) the city is never named, but it feels like either Birmingham or Liverpool to me. It includes a City Hall, a park, a pub called the Four Pennants, a University computer lab (and, by extrapolation, a university), Flat 22 at the Westbrook House flats, and a Hospital.

    The Pridka Dream Centre, in orbit around Taprid, well beyond standardized Earth dating systems, and, seemingly, millennia after the destruction of the solar system (pg 201), thus well beyond the year 5 billion or so. The Dream Centre contains various areas adapted for different aliens as well as its crowning achievement, the Dreamguide.

    IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Some aspects of the plot are very clever (the massive bluff that is Gadrell Major, for example) but others, like the concept of the Infinite Requiem itself and the actual nature of its threat are vague and unfocussed at best. There's also some mind-numbingly awful dialogue, as well as a little too much predictive narrative ('The Director could not possibly have known that this would be a fatal mistake'), but, that said, there are some marvellous moments - the Doctor's entrance on page 57 is a real highlight - and sometimes a lovely use of language. The Earth sections are the best bits, though, and these sadly vanish halfway through. Basically, as the book went on, I found I didn't really care for any of the characters, or the situation or, indeed, very much at all. In the end, it's a story reasonably well-told, but not necessarily worth the telling.
