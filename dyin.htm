The Dying Days
by Lance Parkin

Publisher: Virgin
ISBN: 0 426 20504 9

     

    BASIC PLOT
     Whilst Ice Warriors help a right-wing cabal take control of the United Kingdom, the new Doctor meets the new star of the NAs and then vanishes from the plot for a hundred pages to prove that they can continue without him.

    DOCTOR
     Eighth.

    COMPANIONS
     Benny

    Brigadier Alistair Gordon Lethbridge-Stewart

    "It is over twenty years since the first missions to the Red Planet." The last UK-based mission to Mars was in The Ambassadors of Death.

    Pg 9 "'Where's Chris?' 'Gallifrey.'" Where he remained after Lungbarrow.

Shut them down, by midnight tonight." This will be Virgin Publishing again, appropriately enough as, in essence, their publication of the New Adventures shut down with this book.

Her visit occurred in Blood Harvest, and it's the Seal of Rassilon, initially from The Deadly Assassin, but used frequently since and, as she notes here, was rather overdone in the console room redesign for the telemovie.

    Pg 32 "'OK.' He hesitated for a moment, racking his brains. 'Katherine, with a "K", the same surname." Although it's not been stated yet, the surname is Lethbridge-Stewart, so this is Kate Stewart, seen in the MAs in The Scales of Injustice and Downtime and now heavily in the new series, starting with The Power of Three.

    Pg 47 "Richard Branson and Alan Yentob were arguing about something." Yentob, at the time this book was published, was controller of BBC2, and thus responsible for rebroadcasting Doctor Who on a Friday night during the early 1990s and commissioning documentaries about it. He was, at the time, about the only positive voice about Doctor Who in the entirety of the BBC. Richard Branson is the founder of Virgin, who published this very book. So they were likely arguing about the state of Doctor Who.

    CONTINUITY COCK-UPS

    1. Pg 28 "I used the second console room for so long I got used to all that white, I have to admit, but this always was the original." Erm, no. Lungbarrow makes it quite clear that this console room has come about as a result of the TARDIS interfacing with the House of Lungbarrow in that novel.
    2. Pg 45 "An hour and a half ago, Alexander Christian escaped." Nope. This seems to be happening in real time, and Pg 41 was 10.30. Christian's helicopter crashed soon after 8.00 in the morning, and the power of subtraction allows me to tell you that that was two and a half hours ago.
    3. Pg 65 "Mars Orbiter, this is London Control. Time eleven-oh-eight GMT." Why is it Greenwich Mean Time? It's May, which means Britain is operating in BST, British Summer Time (as, indeed, it turns out we are by Pg 164). You could understand the timing of the Mars mission being done in GMT, as it's hardly British Summer Time on Mars, but the whole country appears to be operating on it: the guests were ushered in at 10.30, just ahead of the landing, for example.
    4. Pg 77 "'That's what Miss Grant called them.' 'Josephine Grant?'" Alexander Christian hears the phrase 'Miss Grant' and instantly - and correctly, remarkably - identifies who it is, despite Grant being an incredibly common surname and him having precisely no knowledge that the girl he once knew was ever connected to UNIT. Given that he's already told his story about aliens to the Brigadier with no idea of the fact that the Brigadier knew anything about aliens - thus coincidentally coming to the one man in the country who could possibly help him - he's doing damn well with the coincidences.
    5. Pg 91 "Miss Summerfield is a friend of the Doctor's from a long time ago." The only time that Brigadier and Bernice ever met was No Future, but, since the Doctor placed mental blocks erasing that adventure in the Brigadier's mind then and only removed them in Happy Endings, which is in the Brig's personal future, how does he remember her?
    6. Pg 174 A disparaging comment on humans from an Ice Warrior: "They are cattle, my Lord." Which is all well and good but, given that this follows on from a whole sequence about how weird humans are and how unlikely the existence of oxygen-breathers is from a Martian perspective, the use of a word to describe a collection of bovine oxygen-breathers is somewhat odd.
    7. Pg 177 "He had been here a full Earth day, and grown almost used to weighing three times more than was normal, but combined with the temperature and the smell and the air like soup, it was all too much." Xznaal's reaction to being on Earth is very well-written, as he stumbles and falls all over the lawn whilst suffering from Martian heat-stroke, but doesn't really gel with the first two times we saw Ice Warriors on Earth. This is most notable in The Seeds of Death, where a whole cohort beam down to Earth, instantly acclimatize and start shooting people, releasing spores and playing 'Shoot-the-Doctor' in a fun-filled game at the Weather Control Centre.
    8. Pg 196 "There was nothing here but memories and alcohol." Benny winds up in the room that she and Jason had shared in the house at Allen Road, which has, unsurprisingly, industrial quantities of vodka in it from when they were co-habiting there. If Ace's room is unstocked and free of anything Ace-like, how is it that Benny and Jason have had time to make memories - and leave vodka - here?

    PLUGGING THE HOLES [Fan-wank theorizing of how to fix continuity cock-ups]

    1. The Doctor senses Benny's disappointment and unease with the new decor, and is trying to make it sound more intentional than it actually was.
    2. Everything's happening very quickly, so it feels shorter than it actually was.
    3. In celebration of the mission, for the day (known as Mars Day, as Pg 77 makes clear), the whole country went back to GMT so they could be successfully aligned with the timing of the landing, so they didn't miss it. Given that, as mentioned, it's BST the following day, this seems to make sense. As much as an excuse like that makes any sense at all.
    4. Christian has been reading Douglas Adams, and so is aware of the fundamental interconnectedness of all things. Once he understands that the Brigadier was the best possible choice that he could make, when he hears the name 'Miss Grant', he realizes that there's only one possible person that it could be.
    5. The phraseology that the Brigadier uses just about allows us to get away with this, as he doesn't actually say that he met her. It's possible that he's heard about her or read files which detail her involvement with the Doctor. (It's annoying though, because Parkin seems to spend a lot of his time retconning what he considers to be the mistakes of others, something which would come across as a lot less patronizing if he didn't make the same kind of silly mistakes himself.)
    6. It's presumably a translation convention. That or there are nitrogen-breathing, heavily-carapaced Martian cows up on the red planet.

    8. OK, the straight answer is that they must have done, but it's still a really weird contradiction which (and this is not the only point in the novel that this comes across) suggests that the book was written in a tearing hurry.
    9. Alex really is that good as a soldier, despite having spent the last twenty years in prison.
    10. The latter problem may be a result of it being an American camera. The former... I have no idea how to help you. Could it be because of Greenwich Mean Time?
    11. OK, I'm losing the ability to do this. Maybe Benny missed a whole chunk of Martian history.     IN SUMMARY - Anthony Wilson
     Difficult to approach unemotionally. I wanted something meaningful and summative as the NAs ended and got a runaround instead. Despite weak Ice Warriors and the excision of the most interesting characters about two-thirds of the way through, it's still a good runaround with a good eighth Doctor and a nice setting up of the Benny NAs. But, for the end of the NAs, I really wanted something rather more substantial than this turns out to be.
